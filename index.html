Southern West Virginia’s Unseen Crisis

On Valentine’s Day 2025, Southern West Virginia drowned. Torrential rains, dumping five inches in hours, swelled the Tug Fork River to 48.35 feet in Williamson, its second-highest crest ever. Homes crumbled, roads vanished, and three lives—including a toddler—were lost in McDowell County. Snow followed, trapping survivors in icy ruins. Nearly three months later, the coalfields remain a wasteland of mold, debris, and despair. Photographer Sydnei Tatum’s images, now catching national eyes, reveal the truth: families like Ashley Rutherford’s live in rotting homes, while volunteers like WVU students fill gaps left by absent aid. Governor Patrick Morrisey calls it progress; residents call it abandonment.

West Virginia, a red state that gave Trump 70% of its vote, heard his promise: disasters like Hurricane Helene wouldn’t happen “on my watch.” This is his watch, yet FEMA aid trickles to only four counties, and the state’s flood trust fund sits empty. As CBS News’ Scott MacFarlane joins Sydnei to witness the devastation, the nation begins to see what Charleston ignores. This is a story of broken promises, resilient spirits, and a community fighting to be heard.

Stories of Resilience and Neglect

Ashley Rutherford: A Mother’s Fight in a Ruined Home

Ashley Rutherford, 33, gripped her wheelchair as floodwaters surged into her Berwind home on February 15. Her four children, aged 6 to 14, tried to lift her to safety, but the muddy tide was relentless. Now, her kitchen floor sags, her walls bloom with mold, and her family lives out of plastic bins. “I wake up crying,” she tells Sydnei Tatum, whose photo captures Ashley’s stove, caked in dried mud. FEMA denied her claim, citing “insufficient damage,” and no state aid has arrived. Ashley’s fight is not just for her home but for her children’s future in a county where floods are a way of life.

WVU Students: Heroes from Morgantown

In Morgantown, 250 miles from McDowell’s mud-soaked streets, WVU students saw Sydnei Tatum’s photos and acted. Packing trucks with blankets, food, and shovels, they drove four hours through snow to help. “We couldn’t just watch,” says Sarah, a 20-year-old nursing major. For weeks, they cleared debris, their boots sinking in sludge, their hands raw. Sydnei’s photo of Sarah hauling a ruined couch speaks to their grit. “The state’s nowhere,” Sarah says, “but we’ll keep coming.” Their story is West Virginia’s heart, beating when leaders fail.

Eula Prater: A Widow’s Last Stand

Eula Prater, 82, fled her Williamson home as water breached her door. “I grabbed my Bible and ran,” she says, her voice trembling. Her house, where she raised three children, is now a shell—photos and furniture gone. Living with a neighbor, Eula fears her town’s end as families leave. Sydnei Tatum’s photo of Eula’s porch, collapsed under debris, captures her loss. No state aid has come, and FEMA’s phone line offers only silence. “We’re a family here,” Eula says, “but we’re fading.” Her story is a plea for remembrance.

Latin Appalachian: Feeding Welch’s Soul

Roberto Diaz, owner of Latin Appalachian in Welch, turned his restaurant into a lifeline. With his daughter Abagail, a high school senior, he’s cooked thousands of free meals—arroz con pollo, warm and hearty—for flood victims. “This is home,” Roberto says, stirring a steaming pot. Sydnei Tatum’s photo of Abagail serving a plate to a weary resident glows with hope. No state aid supports their work, yet they persist. “We feed more than bodies,” Abagail says. Their story shows community thriving where government falters.

Media Spotlight

CBS News: Scott MacFarlane Tours with Sydnei Tatum

On May 6, 2025, CBS News correspondent Scott MacFarlane met photographer Sydnei Tatum in McDowell County to witness the flood’s lingering devastation. Walking through Berwind’s muddy streets, MacFarlane saw homes like Ashley Rutherford’s, still uninhabitable, and debris piles untouched since February. Sydnei’s photos guided his tour, putting faces to the crisis. “Her work is waking people up,” MacFarlane said. This national attention underscores the state’s neglect, as West Virginia’s plight finally reaches a broader audience.

MetroNews Television: Sydnei Tatum’s Call to Action

On May 3, 2025, Sydnei Tatum appeared on MetroNews Television, sharing her powerful images of McDowell County’s flood damage. “These are my neighbors,” she said, urging viewers to see the human toll. Her interview, praised on X for its impact, highlighted stories like Eula Prater’s, drawing local and regional support. Sydnei’s work is shifting the narrative, proving the crisis is far from over.
